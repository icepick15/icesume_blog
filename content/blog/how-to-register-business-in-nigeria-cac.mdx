Every serious business in Nigeria should be registered with the Corporate Affairs Commission (CAC) — Nigeria's official body for business registration. A registered business is legally recognised, can open a corporate bank account, bid for contracts, issue invoices, and access formal financing.

The good news: since 2020, CAC registration has been fully digitised through the Company Registration Portal (CRP). You no longer need to visit a CAC office or use an agent for most registration types.

This guide walks you through the full process for the two most common registration types: **Business Name** and **Private Limited Liability Company (Ltd)**.

---

## Which Business Structure Should You Choose?

Before you register, you need to decide on your structure.

### Business Name (Sole Proprietorship or Partnership)
- Simplest and cheapest to register (₦10,000–₦25,000)
- Owned and operated by one person or a partnership
- **No separate legal identity** — you and the business are legally the same entity
- Cannot issue shares or bring in equity investors
- Best for: freelancers, small traders, sole consultants, market traders going legitimate

### Private Limited Liability Company (Ltd)
- More complex and more expensive (₦35,000–₦100,000+ depending on share capital)
- **Separate legal entity** — the company is distinct from its owners
- Owners (shareholders) have limited liability — personal assets are protected from business debts
- Can have 1–50 shareholders
- Can bring in equity investors and issue shares
- Required for many government contracts, partnerships with multinationals, and formal bank lending
- Best for: startups, businesses planning to scale, those seeking investment

**Most Nigerian entrepreneurs starting out should choose a Business Name first** and upgrade to a Limited Liability Company when the business grows. However, if you plan to raise investment or bid for corporate/government contracts immediately, go straight to a Private Limited Company.

## Part 1: Registering a Business Name

### Step 1: Create an Account on the CAC Portal

Visit [cac.gov.ng](https://www.cac.gov.ng) and click on **Company Registration Portal (CRP)**. Create an account with your email address and phone number. You'll receive an OTP for verification.

### Step 2: Name Availability Search

Before filing, search for your proposed business name to ensure it is available.

- Go to **Search** → **Name Availability Search**
- Enter your proposed name
- CAC will show whether the name is available or taken

**Naming rules:**
- Cannot be identical or confusingly similar to an existing registered name
- Cannot include words like "Government," "National," "Federal," or "State" without permission
- Cannot be misleading about the nature of the business

**Tip:** Search multiple name options — have 2–3 alternatives ready in case your first choice is taken.

### Step 3: Complete the Registration Form

Select **Register a Business Name** and fill in:
- Business name and any acronym/abbreviation
- Nature of business (what your business does)
- Address of principal place of business
- Proprietor details (full name, address, BVN, date of birth)
- If a partnership: details of all partners

### Step 4: Upload Required Documents

- Passport photograph of the proprietor
- Valid ID (NIN, international passport, or driver's licence)
- Proof of address (utility bill or bank statement)

### Step 5: Pay the Registration Fee

Fees are paid online through the CAC portal. Business Name registration currently costs approximately **₦10,000** for a sole proprietorship.

### Step 6: Receive Your Certificate

After submission and fee payment, CAC processes the application. Processing time: typically **5–10 business days** for Business Names, though it can take longer. You receive a digital Certificate of Registration and your Business Registration Number (BRN).

## Part 2: Registering a Private Limited Company (Ltd)

### Step 1: Pre-Registration Requirements

You will need:
- **Company name** (proposed — must pass availability search)
- **Minimum 1 director** (maximum 50 shareholders)
- **Minimum share capital**: No minimum currently set by CAC (removed in 2020), but banks typically want at least ₦100,000–₦1,000,000 to open a corporate account
- **Registered address** in Nigeria (can be your home address to start)
- **MEMART** (Memorandum and Articles of Association) — standard template is available on the CAC portal

### Step 2: Name Availability Search

Same as Business Name — search on the CRP portal. For a Limited Company, you can also **reserve** a name for 60 days while you prepare your documents.

### Step 3: Complete the CAC 1.1 Form

The CAC 1.1 is the primary incorporation form. On the CRP portal:
- Select **Incorporate a Company**
- Fill in company details: name, nature of business, registered address, share capital structure
- Enter details of all directors and shareholders (name, address, email, phone, BVN)
- Specify shares held by each shareholder

### Step 4: Prepare MEMART

The Memorandum and Articles of Association (MEMART) governs how the company operates. The CRP portal has a standard template you can use — for most small businesses, the standard template is sufficient.

If your business has specific governance requirements (e.g., you're raising investor money), consult a lawyer to draft custom articles.

### Step 5: Pay Registration Fees

Fees vary based on share capital:
- Share capital up to ₦1,000,000: approximately ₦35,000
- Share capital ₦1,000,001 to ₦500,000,000: sliding scale fee
- Stamp duty also applies

Fees are paid online through the CAC portal via Remita.

### Step 6: Receive Certificate of Incorporation

After submission, CAC reviews and issues:
- **Certificate of Incorporation** (with your CAC Registration Number — RC number)
- **Status Report** (formerly known as Form C02 and C07)
- **Certified True Copy of MEMART**

Keep physical and digital copies of all documents.

## After Registration: Essential Next Steps

### 1. Open a Corporate Bank Account
Your RC number and CAC documents are required to open a corporate account at any Nigerian bank. A corporate account is essential for professional invoicing, receiving transfers, and separating business and personal finances.

### 2. Register for Tax (TIN)
Every registered business in Nigeria must have a Tax Identification Number (TIN) from FIRS. This is now often automatically assigned when you register with CAC, but verify and obtain your TIN through [firs.gov.ng](https://www.firs.gov.ng).

### 3. Register for VAT (if applicable)
If your annual turnover exceeds ₦25 million, you must register for VAT and charge 7.5% VAT on applicable goods and services. Registration is through FIRS.

### 4. Business Premises Permit (Lagos)
If you operate a physical business in Lagos, you need a Business Premises Permit from the Lagos Internal Revenue Service (LIRS). Other states have equivalent requirements.

## Using an Agent vs DIY

**DIY (recommended for most people):** The CAC portal is designed for self-service. Most Nigerians can complete Business Name registration without assistance. For a Private Limited Company, the portal is slightly more complex but still manageable.

**Using an agent (a.k.a. accredited CAC agents):** These are lawyers or registration professionals authorised by CAC. Useful if:
- You have complex shareholding or governance requirements
- You need custom MEMART
- You prefer not to navigate the portal yourself

**Agent fees:** Typically ₦30,000–₦150,000 above the government fees, depending on the service. Avoid agents who charge significantly above this range.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Registering a name without searching first:** If your name is taken, your application will be rejected and you may lose time.
- **Using a home address but expecting client mail there:** Your registered address is public — if you'd prefer privacy, use a virtual office address service.
- **Not keeping your documents:** CAC registration documents are required for bank accounts, contracts, tender submissions. Store certified copies safely.
- **Confusing Business Name with Limited Company:** They are very different legally. If you need liability protection or investor funding, a Business Name is not sufficient.
