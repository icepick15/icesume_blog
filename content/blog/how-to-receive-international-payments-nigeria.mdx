For Nigerian freelancers, remote workers, business owners, and diaspora families, international payments are a daily reality — and a daily headache.

CBN policy changes, dollar scarcity, unofficial rates, platform restrictions, and changing regulations have all made Nigeria one of the more complicated countries in the world for moving money across borders. But 2024 and 2025 brought meaningful progress, and there are now several reliable, legal options.

This guide covers all the main methods for receiving and sending international payments as a Nigerian in 2026 — with costs, speeds, and the honest limitations of each.

---

## Receiving International Payments: Your Options

### 1. Payoneer

**Best for: Freelancers on platforms like Upwork, Fiverr, Toptal, and direct clients**
**Fees: 0% to receive from other Payoneer users; 1–3% for card payments**
**Withdrawal to Nigerian bank: 1–3 business days**

Payoneer is the most widely used platform by Nigerian freelancers and remote workers. It gives you virtual USD, EUR, and GBP bank account details that you can share with international clients for wire transfers.

**How it works:**
1. Sign up at payoneer.com with your Nigerian details
2. Receive a US/EU/UK virtual bank account number
3. Clients send to that account number via bank transfer
4. Funds appear in your Payoneer balance
5. Withdraw to your Nigerian bank account (naira) or a USD domiciliary account

**Payoneer in Nigeria (2026):** Fully operational. Nigerian users can receive payments, hold balances, and withdraw to Nigerian banks. The Payoneer Mastercard is also available for spending from your balance.

---

### 2. Wise (formerly TransferWise)

**Best for: Receiving payments from international clients, converting currencies**
**Fees: 0.4–1.5% on conversion; low and transparent**
**Withdrawal to Nigerian bank: 1–5 business days**

Wise provides mid-market exchange rate conversions — much better than bank rates. You can receive USD, GBP, EUR, and CAD and convert to naira.

**Important limitation:** Wise does not currently support Nigerian naira accounts as a receiving currency directly. To use Wise effectively, you need a USD domiciliary account at a Nigerian bank — withdraw from Wise in USD and receive it in your domiciliary account.

**Best use case:** Receiving from international clients who want to pay via bank transfer and converting at a fair rate.

---

### 3. USD Domiciliary Account (Nigerian Banks)

**Best for: Holding and receiving dollar payments without conversion**
**Fees: Varies by bank (account maintenance, transaction fees)**
**Availability: GTBank, Zenith Bank, Access Bank, First Bank, Stanbic IBTC**

A USD domiciliary account is a bank account denominated in US dollars, held at a Nigerian commercial bank. It is one of the most important financial tools for any Nigerian professional dealing in foreign currency.

**What you can do with it:**
- Receive USD wire transfers from international clients or employers
- Hold USD without converting (protecting against naira devaluation)
- Withdraw as cash in USD (at the branch) or transfer to other domiciliary accounts
- Fund dollar investment accounts (Risevest, Bamboo) from it

**How to open one:**
- Visit any major Nigerian bank branch
- Submit: valid ID (NIN/passport), utility bill, BVN, and passport photograph
- Some banks (GTBank, Zenith) allow online application
- Minimum opening deposit varies: typically $50–$200

**Important:** Inflows from international sources must be through formal channels — SWIFT transfers, Payoneer withdrawal, Wise withdrawal, or employer payments via licensed entities.

---

### 4. Deel

**Best for: Salaried remote employees and contractors with recurring payments**
**Fees: Employer-side fees; contractor receives full amount**
**Withdrawal to Nigerian bank: 2–5 business days**

Deel is a global payroll and compliance platform used by international companies to pay remote workers legally in their home countries. If your employer uses Deel, you receive payment through the platform and can withdraw to a Nigerian bank account in naira or USD.

Deel has become increasingly popular among Nigerian remote workers because it simplifies the compliance side for employers — making companies more willing to hire Nigerian talent.

---

### 5. Grey (Formerly Aboki Africa)

**Best for: Nigerians who want a virtual USD/GBP/EUR account built for Africa**
**Fees: Low conversion fees; some free tiers**
**Regulatory status: Operates under CBN-licensed partner**

Grey is a Nigerian-built platform specifically designed for Africans receiving international payments. It provides virtual USD, GBP, and EUR account numbers and allows Nigerian users to convert and withdraw to their local bank accounts.

Grey has positioned itself as an alternative to Payoneer with lower fees and faster local withdrawal — worth comparing for freelancers who find Payoneer's fees high.

---

### 6. Crypto / Stablecoins (USDT, USDC)

**Legal status: Regulated in Nigeria as of 2024 CBN policy reversal**
**Best for: Clients in countries where traditional banking is difficult**
**Fees: Near-zero for stablecoin transfers; P2P conversion fees vary**

After the CBN reversed its 2021 crypto ban, regulated cryptocurrency exchanges are now operating legally in Nigeria. Some Nigerian freelancers and remote workers receive payment in USDT (Tether) or USDC — US dollar-pegged stablecoins — and convert to naira via P2P exchanges.

**Legal consideration:** Foreign income received in crypto is still taxable. Nigerians receiving crypto income should declare it to FIRS as they would any other foreign income.

**Platforms:** Binance (with Nigerian KYC), Patricia, Yellow Card, and other CBN-compliant exchanges.

## Sending International Payments from Nigeria

Sending money out of Nigeria is generally more restricted than receiving, due to CBN FX controls.

### For personal remittances (supporting family abroad):

**Western Union / MoneyGram:** Available at major Nigerian banks and outlets. Can send from Nigeria to most countries. Fees and rates vary.

**Remitly:** [remitly.com](https://www.remitly.com) — competitive rates for sending naira-equivalent to countries like the UK, US, and Canada.

**Wise:** Sending naira internationally is possible through Wise in some corridors. Check current availability for the specific destination country.

### For business payments (paying international suppliers or contractors):

**SWIFT transfer via your Nigerian bank:** For significant business payments, international wire transfer through a Nigerian bank is the most formal route. Submit the required documentation (invoice, purpose of transfer) to your bank's FX desk.

**Domiciliary account transfer:** Transfer USD from your domiciliary account to an international recipient via SWIFT.

## The Exchange Rate Reality

One of the biggest decisions Nigerian professionals face is when to convert foreign currency earnings to naira.

**Official rate (CBN/NAFEM):** The formal interbank rate.
**Parallel market rate:** The street/unofficial rate, typically higher than official.
**P2P rate (crypto exchanges):** Often tracks between official and parallel.

For most regulated platforms (Payoneer, Wise, bank transfers), you receive the official or near-official rate when converting to naira. The gap between official and parallel rates has narrowed significantly since 2024's FX reforms, but still exists.

**Strategic tip:** If you do not urgently need naira, hold USD in a domiciliary account or Payoneer balance rather than converting immediately. Naira has historically depreciated over time, so holding dollars until you need naira often results in more naira per dollar.

## Tax Obligations on Foreign Income

Nigerian residents who earn income from international sources are required by FIRS to:

1. Declare foreign income in their annual tax return
2. Pay applicable PAYE or Personal Income Tax on the income
3. Apply any relevant tax treaty benefits (Nigeria has double-taxation treaties with several countries)

If you earn significant dollar income as a freelancer or remote worker, consult a Nigerian tax professional. Undeclared foreign income creates legal risk, and FIRS has been increasing enforcement of foreign income tax obligations.
