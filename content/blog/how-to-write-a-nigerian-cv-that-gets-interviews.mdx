Your CV is the only thing standing between you and an interview. Most Nigerians spend hours applying and hear nothing back — not because they are unqualified, but because their CV is not written the way Nigerian (and international) recruiters actually read them.

This guide will show you exactly what a strong Nigerian CV looks like in 2026, what to cut, and what to add.

## The State of Nigerian CV Writing in 2026

Nigerian hiring is changing. Companies like Flutterwave, Paystack, GTBank, MTN Nigeria, and multinationals like Unilever and Nestlé Nigeria now use Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) that scan your CV before a human sees it. If your CV does not pass the ATS scan, no one reads it.

At the same time, Lagos-based tech companies and startups have started hiring more like their Silicon Valley counterparts — they want to see achievements, not just job duties.

The result: a CV that would have worked in 2018 Nigeria will not work in 2026.

## What Nigerian Recruiters Actually Look For

Before anything else, understand what a recruiter in Nigeria is trying to do in the 6 seconds they spend on your CV:

1. **Can this person do the job?** (skills and experience match)
2. **Have they done it before?** (evidence of results, not just duties)
3. **Do they look credible?** (formatting, no typos, clear structure)

That is it. Everything else is secondary.

## The 7 Sections Every Strong Nigerian CV Must Have

### 1. Contact Information (Top of the page)

Include:
- Full name (bold, slightly larger font)
- Phone number (with +234 country code if applying internationally)
- Professional email address (firstname.lastname@gmail.com — never a funny nickname)
- LinkedIn profile URL
- City and State (Lagos, Abuja, Port Harcourt etc. — full address not needed)

**What to remove:** Date of birth, passport photograph, religion, tribe, and marital status. These are legally irrelevant and waste space. Nigerian law does not require them, and many modern companies see them as red flags for bias.

### 2. Professional Summary (4–5 lines)

This is the most important section most Nigerians either skip or write poorly. A professional summary replaces the old "Objective Statement" and it should:

- State who you are professionally
- Mention your years of experience and key area of expertise
- Include one or two specific achievements

**Poor example (common in Nigeria):**
> "A hardworking and dedicated professional seeking an opportunity to utilise my skills and experience in a reputable organisation."

This says nothing. Every CV says this.

**Strong example:**
> "Marketing professional with 5 years of experience driving brand growth for FMCG companies in Nigeria. Led the relaunch of [Brand X] across Lagos and Abuja, increasing retail footprint by 40% in 12 months. Expert in trade marketing, BTL activations, and managing distributor relationships across South-West Nigeria."

See the difference? The second one is specific, credible, and memorable.

### 3. Work Experience (Reverse Chronological Order)

List your most recent job first. For each role, include:
- Job title
- Company name and location
- Duration (Month/Year — Month/Year)
- 3–5 bullet points of **achievements**, not duties

**The key shift Nigerian CVs must make:** Stop listing duties. Start listing results.

**Duty (weak):**
> "Responsible for managing social media accounts"

**Achievement (strong):**
> "Grew company Instagram from 2,000 to 18,000 followers in 8 months through targeted content strategy and influencer collaborations, resulting in a 25% increase in online inquiries"

Use numbers wherever possible. Nigerian employers respond to:
- Revenue generated or saved (in NGN or USD)
- Percentages of growth or improvement
- Team sizes managed
- Timelines (e.g., "delivered project 3 weeks ahead of schedule")

### 4. Education

List your most recent qualification first:
- Degree and classification (e.g., B.Sc. Accounting, 2nd Class Upper)
- University name
- Graduation year
- NYSC discharge year

For NYSC, just list: "NYSC — [State of Deployment], [Year]" — no need to elaborate unless the service was relevant.

**Important:** Do not list O'Level results if you have a degree. Space is precious.

### 5. Skills Section

Split this into two parts:

**Technical skills:** Software, tools, certifications, programming languages
- Example: Advanced Excel, Power BI, Python, Salesforce, QuickBooks

**Industry knowledge:** Domain expertise
- Example: Trade marketing, FMCG distribution, KYC compliance, credit risk analysis

Avoid generic "soft skills" like "team player" and "communication skills." Every CV says this. Let your achievements demonstrate them instead.

### 6. Professional Certifications

This is increasingly important in Nigeria in 2026. List:
- ICAN, ACCA, CFA, CIMA (finance)
- PMP, PRINCE2 (project management)
- AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure (tech)
- CIPM, SHRM (HR)
- Any relevant Google, HubSpot, or Coursera certifications

Certifications signal continuous learning and are highly valued by Nigerian and multinational employers.

### 7. LinkedIn and Portfolio (Optional but Recommended)

In 2026, many Nigerian tech and creative roles expect a LinkedIn profile and/or portfolio link:
- LinkedIn: Ensure your profile is 90%+ complete
- Developers: GitHub profile link
- Designers: Behance or personal portfolio
- Writers: Published articles or content samples

## Common Mistakes on Nigerian CVs (And How to Fix Them)

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Passport photograph on CV | Remove it — modern companies reject this |
| "To obtain a position..." objective | Replace with a professional summary |
| 3+ pages long | Keep it to 1–2 pages max |
| Listing duties instead of achievements | Use numbers and results |
| Generic email address (e.g., bigboy2000@) | Use firstname.lastname@gmail.com |
| Inconsistent formatting | Pick one clean font, one size, consistent bullet style |
| Listing every WAEC subject | Remove if you have a degree |
| No LinkedIn profile | Create and complete one |
| Font: Times New Roman, size 10 | Use Calibri, Arial, or Georgia, size 11 |

---

## Nigerian CV Formatting Rules

- **Length:** 1 page if under 3 years experience, 2 pages maximum for senior roles
- **Font:** Calibri, Arial, or Garamond — size 11 body, 13–14 for name
- **Margins:** 1.5–2cm on all sides
- **File format:** Always submit as PDF (never Word — formatting breaks)
- **File name:** "FirstName_LastName_CV.pdf" — never "My CV Final Final.pdf"

## ATS Optimisation for Nigerian Applications

Many large Nigerian companies and all multinationals now use ATS software. To pass ATS:

1. **Use the job description keywords** — if the JD says "financial modelling," your CV must use that exact phrase
2. **Use standard section headings** — "Work Experience" not "Career Journey"
3. **No tables or text boxes** — ATS cannot read them
4. **No graphics or logos** embedded in the CV
5. **Simple bullet points** — not custom symbols

You should **never** include salary expectation on your Nigerian CV unless specifically requested. It anchors the negotiation too early. Wait until the interview stage.
