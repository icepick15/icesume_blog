Most Nigerian professionals leave money on the table every time they take a job. They receive an offer, feel grateful, and say yes immediately — without negotiating.

This is understandable. Nigerian workplace culture carries an unspoken fear that negotiating will make you look ungrateful, greedy, or risk losing the offer altogether. But here is what experienced hiring managers actually know: **almost every offer has room to move, and most recruiters expect you to negotiate.**

This guide gives you the strategy and the exact words to use — for email, phone calls, and in-person conversations in a Nigerian context.

---

## Why Nigerians Don't Negotiate (And Why That's Costing You)

A ₦50,000 difference in monthly salary is ₦600,000 per year. Over five years, that is ₦3,000,000 — before you factor in pension contributions, annual raises calculated as a percentage of your base, and the compounding effect on your next salary negotiation.

The fear is real, but the risk is almost always overstated. In ten-plus years of hiring across Nigerian companies, it is extremely rare for an employer to rescind an offer because a candidate negotiated professionally. What actually happens when you don't negotiate is simpler: you earn less, potentially for years.

## Before You Negotiate: Know Your Number

Negotiating without knowing the market rate is guesswork. Before you say a word, do this research:

**1. Check Jobberman's salary data**
Search your exact job title on Jobberman — they publish salary ranges by role and experience level for the Nigerian market.

**2. Ask in your professional network**
The most accurate salary data comes from people doing your job at similar companies. One honest conversation with a peer can tell you more than any salary website.

**3. Use the iCesume Salary Calculator**
Our [Nigerian Salary Calculator](/salary-calculator) shows you take-home pay at different gross salaries — useful when comparing offers, since a ₦400,000 gross at one company and ₦420,000 at another may feel similar but have different net values after deductions.

**4. Factor in company type**
- **Multinational in Nigeria** (MTN, Unilever, Nestlé, TotalEnergies): typically pay 30–60% more than local equivalents
- **Nigerian bank (Tier 1)**: Zenith, GTBank, Access, First Bank — structured, grade-based salaries, moderate negotiation room
- **Startup / fintech**: more flexible on base, sometimes offer equity
- **Government / public sector**: salary scales are fixed, little room to negotiate base (focus on allowances)

**Your target number**: identify a range. Your ideal figure is the top of the range. Your walk-away point is the minimum you'll accept. Never reveal either of these in the negotiation — but know them clearly before you start.

## The Core Principle: Negotiate the Package, Not Just the Salary

In Nigeria, your total compensation often includes elements beyond base salary:

- **Housing allowance** (very common in Nigerian companies, often 15–25% of basic)
- **Transport allowance**
- **Meal subsidy**
- **Annual leave allowance**
- **13th month / end-of-year bonus**
- **Health insurance** (quality varies enormously)
- **Pension** (employer contribution above the legal 10% minimum)
- **Professional development budget**
- **Remote work or hybrid arrangement**

If they can't move on base salary, you can negotiate these. A ₦50,000 transport allowance or a fully paid HMO plan is real money.

## When to Bring Up Salary

**Not in the first interview.** If a recruiter asks your salary expectation in an early screening call, deflect:

> *"I'm more focused on understanding the role fully at this stage. I'd love to discuss compensation once we've both confirmed this is a strong fit — is that okay?"*

**After you have the offer in writing.** This is your strongest position. You have been selected. They want you. The power has shifted.

If pushed to give a number before an offer, give a range — with the bottom of your range being the number you'd actually accept:

> *"Based on my research and experience, I'm targeting between ₦380,000 and ₦450,000 gross monthly. I'm open to discussing based on the full package."*

## Script 1: Negotiating a Written Job Offer by Email

This is the safest and most common method in Nigerian companies. Email gives you time to think, and gives them time to consult internally.

**Template:**

> Subject: Re: Job Offer — [Your Name] — [Role Title]
>
> Dear [Recruiter/HR Name],
>
> Thank you so much for the offer letter for the [Role Title] position. I'm genuinely excited about the opportunity and I believe I can make a strong contribution to the team from day one.
>
> After carefully reviewing the offer, I'd like to respectfully discuss the base salary. Based on my [X years of experience] in [relevant area], and the market research I've done for this role in Lagos, I was expecting something closer to ₦[your target figure] gross monthly.
>
> I understand budget constraints are real, and I'm flexible. If the base salary has limited room to move, I'd be happy to explore other parts of the package — such as [housing allowance / performance bonus / professional development budget].
>
> I'm very motivated to join [Company Name] and I'm confident we can find a structure that works for both of us. Could we schedule a brief call to discuss?
>
> Kind regards,
> [Your Name]

**What makes this work:**
- It opens with genuine enthusiasm — you're not threatening to walk
- It gives a specific number (vague requests get vague responses)
- It offers flexibility (alternative compensation)
- It frames it as a conversation, not a demand

## Script 2: Negotiating Over the Phone or In Person

Phone and in-person negotiations happen faster and require you to think on your feet. Practice this out loud before the call.

**Opening:**

> *"Thank you for the offer — I'm very interested in the role. Before I formally accept, would it be okay if we discussed the compensation package briefly?"*

Almost everyone will say yes. If they say the offer is final, go to Script 4.

**The ask:**

> *"I've done my research on market rates for this role in Lagos, and my experience in [specific skill/achievement] puts me at the higher end of the range. I was hoping we could get to ₦[target] on the base. Is there any flexibility there?"*

Then **stop talking.** This is the hardest part. Many Nigerians fill the silence with justifications that weaken their position. Make your ask, then wait.

**If they say yes or "let me check":**

> *"That's great — thank you. I'd appreciate getting the updated offer in writing. I'm ready to confirm my acceptance once I receive it."*

**If they come back with a smaller increase than you wanted:**

> *"I appreciate you working on that. Could we meet in the middle at ₦[figure between their counter and your ask]?"*

---

## Script 3: When You Have a Competing Offer

A competing offer is the single most powerful negotiating tool you have. Use it carefully and honestly — don't fabricate one.

> *"I want to be transparent with you — I have another offer currently at ₦[figure]. Your company is genuinely my first choice because of [specific reason — the role, the team, the industry]. If you're able to match or get close to that figure, I would accept immediately."*

**Important:** Only use this if the competing offer is real and you would genuinely consider taking it. Using a fake competing offer is not just unethical — it can damage your reputation in Nigeria's relatively small professional networks, especially in sectors like banking, oil and gas, or tech where people know each other.

---

## Script 4: When They Say "The Offer Is Final"

This happens, especially at large structured Nigerian employers — banks, FMCG multinationals, and government-owned enterprises. Salary grades are rigid. But the package often isn't.

> *"I understand — thank you for being upfront about that. In that case, I'd love to explore whether there's flexibility on other parts of the package. Specifically, I was thinking about [the transport allowance / a signing bonus / an earlier performance review / remote work days]. Would any of those be possible?"*

A signing bonus is particularly effective here — it's a one-time cost for the company, not a permanent increase, so finance teams are often more willing to approve it.

## Script 5: Negotiating a Raise at Your Current Job

This is different from negotiating a new offer — you're asking for recognition of the value you've already delivered.

**Timing matters enormously.** The best times to ask in a Nigerian company:
- Just after a major win or project success
- During your annual performance review
- When you've been given additional responsibilities
- When you have a competing external offer (real only)

**The conversation:**

> *"I've been reflecting on my contributions over the past [X months/year], and I'd like to discuss my compensation. In that time, I've [specific achievement — led the project that brought in X clients / reduced processing time by Y% / managed the new product launch]. I believe this reflects a higher level of contribution than when my salary was last reviewed. I was hoping we could discuss bringing my compensation to ₦[figure]."*

**Document everything before this conversation.** Write down your achievements with numbers. A raise request with evidence is five times more likely to succeed than a vague "I deserve more."

## What Not to Say in a Nigerian Salary Negotiation

These phrases weaken your position:

| Don't say | Why | Say instead |
|---|---|---|
| "I really need this job" | Destroys your leverage | Stay neutral on urgency |
| "My rent is due" | Personal finances are irrelevant to your market value | Focus on your contribution |
| "I know this is a lot to ask, but..." | Undermines you before you've even asked | State your ask directly |
| "I'll take anything" | Signals you have no alternatives | Always have a walk-away point |
| "My last salary was ₦X" | Anchors the conversation to the past | Anchor to market rate and your value |

## After the Negotiation

Whether you got everything you asked for or not:

1. **Get everything in writing** before you resign from your current role
2. **Don't burn bridges** if you decline — Nigerian industries are small and people move around
3. **If they didn't move on salary**, ask for a 90-day review with a specific target — *"Could we agree to revisit this after 90 days once I've had the chance to demonstrate my contribution?"*
4. **Calculate your real take-home** on the agreed figure using our [Salary Calculator](/salary-calculator) before signing

## The Mindset Shift

Negotiating your salary is not disrespectful in Nigeria — it is professional. The companies that rescind offers because a candidate negotiated politely are not companies you want to work for.

The hiring manager who made you an offer went to their leadership and said "this is the person we want." They are invested in closing you. A respectful, evidence-based negotiation almost never destroys that. What destroys it is being unprepared, making ultimatums, or negotiating with emotion instead of data.

Know your number. Know the market. Use the scripts. And ask.
